## Trump extends deadline for Iran to reopen strait

Trump said on Monday that he had extended his 48-hour deadline for Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz. The president had previously threatened to strike nuclear plants if Iran refused to open the pivotal waterway that carries one-fifth of the world’s oil supply, by Monday evening.

Iran responded with further threats to target energy and water facilities in the region if Trump followed through with the attack.

Trump said he would give Iran five more days and that Tehran wanted “to settle, and we’re going to get it done, I hope.” However, Iranian state media reported that the country’s foreign ministry denied being in official talks with the US.

## Netanyahu and Trump ‘discussed assassination of supreme leader’ before strikes

President Trump launched the war on Iran less than 48 hours after a phone conversation with Binyamin Netanyahu, in which the Israeli prime minister argued that the timing was on their side to plot the assassination of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei.

During the call, Netanyahu said it would be a good opportunity to avenge previous efforts by Iran to assassinate Trump, three people briefed on the call told Reuters.

Both Trump and Netanyahu agreed to launch the joint operation on February 28 after obtaining intelligence that Khamenei and his key lieutenants had expedited a previously scheduled meeting, making a “decapitation strike” by the US and Israel more likely to succeed.

## How many missiles does Iran have left?

At the beginning of the conflict with Israel and the US, Iran had about 2,500 ballistic missiles that could be fired either from the back of trucks or from underground silos, according to the Alma Research Centre, an Israeli think tank. That has been reduced to about 1,000 missiles, the centre estimates in a new report.

Yet Iran has shown resilience before. Alma assesses that at the end of the 12-day war last year Iran was left with about 1,500 missiles, but in the eight months that followed it was able to manufacture another 1,000 — suggesting Tehran may be able to replenish its stockpiles quickly despite the US and Israeli bombing campaign targeting its military-industrial complex.

## Move at UN to protect Strait of Hormuz

Bahrain has put forward a draft United Nations security council resolution that would authorise countries to use “all necessary means” — diplomatic language for force — to protect commercial shipping in and around the Strait of Hormuz.

The draft resolution calls Iran’s actions a threat to international peace and security and was backed by other Gulf Arab states and the US, diplomats said, although they thought it unlikely to succeed.

The text would authorise countries, acting alone or through voluntary multinational naval coalitions, to use “all necessary means” in and around the strait, including in the territorial waters of littoral states, to ensure passage and to prevent moves to interfere with international navigation.

## Price of metal used in missiles surges

The price of tungsten has surged since the start of the conflict due to it being used in armour-piercing missiles. In recent months it has outpaced the price rises for copper, gold and oil.

China accounts for about 80 per cent of global mined production, and supply has been tightened since Beijing imposed export controls in February 2025 in response to American tariffs.

The Rotterdam market for ammonium paratungstate (APT), an intermediate product used to make tungsten metal, has surged from under $400 per metric tonne a year ago to more than $2,200, according to the Chinese price reporting agency Shanghai Metals Market.

The Pentagon sought fresh supplies of 13 critical minerals including tungsten from industry partners the day before American and Israeli strikes on Iran began.

According to the consultancy firm Project Blue, citing US Geological Survey data, tungsten products are trading at their highest level in at least 90 years. As military buyers look to replenish their supplies, it could put pressure on manufacturers who use tungsten for civilian products such as semiconductors, circuit boards and solar panels.
