Nervous investors are desperately seeking signs that the US president is headed for an off-ramp, but the latest events are not evidence that a ceasefire is close.

_Washington_ | It was clear from the way that markets gripped tightly to the relief offered by Donald Trump’s de-escalation in Iran – even in the face of “fake news” denials from Tehran – that investors were looking for a breakthrough.

And fair enough. The prospect of US forces taking out Iranian power plants – worth $US10 billion a pop to build, according to Trump – had so rattled what was left of the Islamic Republic regime that it had threatened to retaliate with devastating strikes on critical infrastructure and tourism spots across the Middle East.

![Image 5](https://static.ffx.io/images/$zoom_0.495%2C$multiply_2%2C$ratio_1.5%2C$width_756%2C$x_0%2C$y_0/t_crop_custom/c_scale%2Cw_620%2Cq_88%2Cf_auto/fce620356eb2cda62feccc4ccd4be2022f8aeec3)

Donald Trump is always negotiating. “My life is a deal, that’s all I do is deals, my whole life,” he told reporters.AP

This rhetorical tit-for-tat was due to come to a head on Monday night (10.44am Tuesday AEDT), but was avoided when the president, predictably, hit pause via a social media post before US markets opened.

He walked back his threat of bombing Iran’s electricity infrastructure and offered five more days for Iran to make a deal. The gesture jolted [equity markets higher](https://www.afr.com/markets/equity-markets/wall-street-surges-on-trump-triggered-relief-rally-oil-plunges-12pc-20260324-p5t14r), driven largely by an immediate 13 per cent fall in oil prices that only clawed back a fraction when Iran denied that talks were taking place.

But nervous investors, desperately seeking signs Trump is heading for an off-ramp, should not fall into the trap of seeing Monday’s events as evidence of steps towards a near-term ceasefire.

Advertisement

There seems to be a dangerous optimism in the way the market has hung onto the promise in his early morning post. There were plenty of inferences of further conflict and hostility in other words he uttered throughout the day (not to mention his near-life-long and fairly consistent vow to take down the threat of Iran).

Indeed, in a rapid-fire round of questions with reporters in Florida, he gave at least three signs of being far from finished with this war.

First, it is important to remember that Trump is always negotiating. “My life is a deal, that’s all I do is deals, my whole life,” he said.

Revelling in the way that his threat to the Iranian power grid had landed, the president boasted that Iran had been the first to call the US to discuss the terms of any deal.

He also refused to discuss the role that a task force of warships and 4000 Marines en route to the Middle East will play. While it is still some time away from the region, a separate fleet sent from Asia will arrive in the coming days, and the looming presence of both feels like a strengthening of the US hand before Trump makes his next move.

Advertisement

Second, as Trump laid out what he wants from the negotiations with Iran, he appears to have tweaked two objectives. On halting Iran’s ability to create a nuclear weapon, he has indicated an appetite to put US forces on the ground to collect any enriched uranium from the underground bunkers his B2 bombers “obliterated” last June.

![Image 6](https://static.ffx.io/images/$zoom_0.151%2C$multiply_4%2C$ratio_1.5%2C$width_756%2C$x_0%2C$y_0/t_crop_custom/c_scale%2Cw_620%2Cq_88%2Cf_auto/32538e03379284c6236871488130803bf9682153)

People wave flags in support of the Iranian government in central Tehran at the weekend. Getty

“If we have a deal with them, we’re going down, and we’ll take it ourselves,” he said.

While that presents a hardening of what stopping Iran’s nuclear ambitions looks like, he appears open to the regime maintaining some firepower. After spending the first three weeks of the war saying the US wanted to knock out Iran’s military strike capability, on Monday, he toned that down to wanting to see the regime “low key in the missiles”.

It is risky to over-interpret Trump’s semantic shifts, but given his propensity for dropping clues when in the throes of deal making it can be useful.

The US has maintained other pre-war demands that Iran stop funding regional proxy groups such as Hamas in Gaza and Hezbollah in Lebanon, while Iran has demanded that the US close its military bases in the region, pay compensation and commit to a new legal regime for the Strait of Hormuz.

Advertisement

Macquarie global rates strategist Thierry Wizman said in a note to clients on Monday that despite the outbreak of optimism, the demands of both sides seemed “unbridgeable” and “far-fetched”. Wizman expects at least another fortnight of combat operations until mid-April, by which time Iran’s “war-making” capabilities will be degraded enough to give the US the upper hand in negotiations.

A third factor is that Trump again signalled he would not be satisfied until he has secured the Strait of Hormuz. Trump and his team have already indicated one option would be [ramping up strikes](https://www.afr.com/link/follow-20180101-p5rmj7) on the Iranian coastal side of the waterway to clear a path for maritime traffic.

And on Monday, he almost offhandedly suggested such a feat would be achieved when it was controlled jointly by the US and Iran.

That would require Iran to come to the table during this period of talks and an extraordinary level of co-operation between two long-running foes. And in the highly possible case that does not occur, and a broad agreement cannot be reached? “We’ll just keep bombing our little hearts out,” Trump says.

We shall see in five days.
